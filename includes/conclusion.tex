In this thesis, we explored multiple use cases of \glspl{llm} for local
\gls{cli} assistance under tight resource constraints. The first use case was
command vs.\ natural language classification for seamless shell integration.
Across a balanced \texttt{classifier-100} dataset, accuracy ranged from 50\% to
88\%, with the best model requiring 2.9s per query and smaller models operating
near chance-level. These results show that local \glspl{llm} are not reliable
enough for real-time classification.

The second use case was document retrieval with embedding \glspl{llm} over local
man pages. Three approaches were evaluated: document-level retrieval, document
retrieval with passage-level re-ranking, and passage-level retrieval. On
\texttt{sample-10} (14 documents, 250 queries), document-level Top-1 accuracy
was 83.6--86.8\% with Top-5 up to 100\% and MRR up to 0.916. On
\texttt{sample-100} (101 documents, 300 queries), Top-1 was 62.7--72.7\% with
Top-5 up to 92.0\% and MRR up to 0.806. Granite embeddings delivered the lowest
per-query latency (about 0.04--0.10s) and fast index builds (1.6--2.3s), while
qwen3-embedding required 128--134s to build indices on the larger corpus.
Passage-level re-ranking increased latency to 0.6--3.8s and reduced Top-1
accuracy (39.2--55.2\% on \texttt{sample-10}, 42.7--45.7\% on
\texttt{sample-100}) without improving Top-5. Passage-level retrieval matched
document-level precision with low latency (0.03--0.10s), making it a practical
alternative without re-ranking overhead.

The third use case was \gls{llm}-based summarization of retrieved content.
Summarization produced concise responses when retrieval was accurate, but it was
sensitive to retrieval quality and context size. Overall, the results show that a
retrieval-first architecture is viable for local \gls{cli} assistance, while
classification-heavy and agentic approaches remain impractical on small models.

In most use cases, the embedding model of size under 0.6b parameters is
sufficient for retrieval in such a constrained environment, the generation model
of size under 2b proved to be sufficient for summarization, while for
classification, chosen approach proved to be inefficient, and other, more
efficient approaches exist.
