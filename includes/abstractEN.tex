This thesis examines whether a practical Linux \gls{cli} assistant can be built
with small local \glspl{llm} under strict offline and privacy constraints. A
survey of existing assistants shows strong dependence on remote APIs and poor
reliability of agentic workflows on local models. In response, the thesis
proposes shAI, a modular \gls{rag}-based prototype composed of a command-vs.-query
classifier, a document retriever that indexes Linux man pages, and a summarizer
that generates concise answers from retrieved passages. The implementation uses
Ollama for local inference and \gls{faiss} for vector indexing.

Evaluation is component-focused. The classifier was tested on the balanced
\texttt{classifier-100} dataset; accuracy ranged from 50\% to 88\%, with the best
model requiring 2.9s per query. The retriever was evaluated on \texttt{sample-10}
(14 documents, 250 queries) and \texttt{sample-100} (101 documents, 300 queries).
Document-level retrieval achieved Top-5 accuracy up to 100\% and 92\%
respectively, with low per-query latency for Granite embeddings. Passage-level
re-ranking increased latency (0.6--3.8s) and reduced Top-1 accuracy, while
passage-level retrieval matched document-level precision with low latency.

The results demonstrate that retrieval-first architectures are practical for
local \gls{cli} assistance, whereas classification-heavy and agentic approaches
remain unreliable on small models. The thesis provides an open-source prototype
and empirical benchmarks that guide future work on model optimization and
constrained task design.
