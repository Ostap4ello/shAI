This thesis investigates whether a practical Linux \gls{cli} assistant can be
built with small local \glspl{llm} under strict offline and privacy constraints.
After surveying existing assistants, the work identifies heavy reliance on
remote APIs and limited suitability of agentic workflows for local models. It
proposes shAI, a modular \gls{rag}-based prototype composed of a
command-vs.-query classifier, a document retriever that indexes Linux man pages,
and a summarizer that generates concise answers from retrieved passages. The
implementation uses Ollama for local inference and \gls{faiss} for vector
indexing.

Evaluation is component-focused. The classifier was tested on a balanced\linebreak
\texttt{classifier-100} dataset; the best model achieved 88\% accuracy but
required 2.9s per query, while smaller models remained near chance-level. The
retriever was evaluated on \texttt{sample-10} (14 documents, 250 queries) and
\texttt{sample-100} (101 documents, 300 queries). Document-level retrieval
achieved Top-5 accuracy up to 100\% and 92\% respectively, with low per-query
latency for Granite embeddings; passage re-ranking increased latency without
improving Top-1 accuracy.

The results show that embedded-LLM-focused architectures are practical for local
\gls{cli} assistance, while  agentic and open-ended approaches remain unreliable
on small models. The thesis delivers an open-source prototype and empirical
guidance for future work on model optimization and constrained tasks.
