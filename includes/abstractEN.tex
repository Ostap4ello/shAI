This thesis examines whether a practical Linux \gls{cli} assistant can be built
with small local \glspl{llm} under offline and hardware constraints. A survey of
existing assistants was conducted and it shows strong dependence on remote APIs
and poor reliability of agentic workflows on local models. In response, the
thesis proposes shAI, a modular \gls{rag}-based prototype composed of a
command-vs.-query classifier, a document retriever that indexes Linux man pages,
and a summarizer that generates concise answers from retrieved passages. The
implementation uses Ollama for local inference and \gls{faiss} for vector
indexing.

Evaluation is component-focused. The classifier was tested on the balanced
\texttt{classifier-100} dataset; accuracy and latency of this approach hass
proved inefficient, as ranged from 50\% to 88\%, with the best model requiring
2.9s per query. The retriever was evaluated on \texttt{sample-10} (14 documents,
250 queries) and \texttt{sample-100} (101 documents, 300 queries) with three
different approaches, each with different accuracy and latency trade-offs,
overall reaching up to 90\% Top-1 accuracy while keeping latency under 1.5s. The
end-to-end system latencies were measured with the lightest and the heaviest
models, showing that the system can provide answers in under 2s and 10s

The results demonstrate that strictly defined (for example, retrieval
architectures) are practical for local \gls{cli} assistance, whereas agentic
open-ended approaches remain unreliable on small models. The thesis provides an
open-source prototype and empirical benchmarks that guide future work on model
optimization and constrained task design.
